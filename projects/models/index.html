Case Study

Label your Data, Synthetically

Product DesignUX Research

A platform for machine learning scientists that enables the creation of predictive models for text and image categorization.

The Problem

As A Data Scientist, I am sending all my unlabeled data to costly human annotators to classify. I want a solution that lets me first send data to a machine learning model to classify. By doing this I can employ less annotators and reduce cost.

Overview

Data scientists come to the platform with raw image and text data and receive categorizations of the data in return. For example, one image might get labeled as having a tree. Another image might get labeled as having a street sign.

The basic path on the platform is to send the data to human annotators for categorizing. There is signifigant expense to do this at the scale data scientists need.

Our goal was to integrate a step into the process in which a user can employ machine learning to predict the categories. With a portion of the data categorized synthetically, the user can reduce the amount of data that gets sent to human annotators, thus reducing costs.

Imagine that a data scientist needs 10,000 images labeled. A machine learning model predicts (with high confidence) that 40% of the images absolutley contain a street sign. The remaining 60% of low-confidence images can then go to human annotators for their expertise.

This approach makes the machine learning training process more efficient, affordable and scalable, without reducing quality. We created an experience to stream data in this way!

Users and Audience

The users for this new feature are primarily data scientists. Secondary users include machine learning project managers and internal pro service team members. Throughout the design process we referenced three personas that had previously been created, representing a typical customer machine learning team.

My Role

I served as the lead designer on this project. I worked closely with a project manager and both front-end and back-end engineers to understand scope as the project progressed.

Length: 4 months with ongoing enhancements.

Design Process

Initial Discovery

Sponsored Users

We kicked off this process by gathering sponsored users. This ultimately helped us to develop initial user stories, product requirements, and initial wireframe sketches. Because we set out to build a completely new offering on our platform, working closely with our sponsored users at every step of the design process was essential for staying focused and on track.

We talked to our customers about the work that goes into training good machine learning models, a process that is both time consuming and costly. We learned about what kind of models they employ or would hope to employ and the facets of their data. We discussed what form their raw data is in and what would be the optimal output once the data is turned into information on our platform.

Our primary sponsored user wanted a faster and more cost effective way to categorize fashion images in particular. They provided us with some of their unlabeled data and we used the data in our prototypes to outfit the experience according to their use case. This helped us ensure that we were building the right feature for them and other teams with a similar use case.

Ideation

Many of the challenges around this design were focused on trust and safety and the 'UX of machine learning'; How do we cut corners with machine learning and retain trust? We referenced our previosuly developed Design Principles to guide the design and justify some of the design decisions.

Because the process of getting training data and using it to train a model is complicated, a good amount of time was spent diagramming the experience and the user flow prior to more formal prototyping. We wanted to make a complicated process as simple and painless as possible.

Retrospective and Lessons Learned

Understanding power users

This project brought about extra challenges in getting into the heads of our primary user, the machine learning scientist. In some use cases a designer can get pretty far armed with interaction best practices, a design system, and their own experiences using technology. For this project, I had to go more in depth on how machine learning works, the terminology used, and what input and output information is crucial to the experience of creating a machine learning model.

The overhead knowledge required to work with machine learning models appears complicated and technical from my own point of view. This of course isn't the case for a skilled machine learning scientist. I found it challenging to design an experience that meets the user at a level of knowledge I didn't have myself.

To alleviate this, I did my research and made an effort to 'know what I don't know'. Working closely with sponsored users and our own in-house machine learning team was invaluable. Because they were part of the design process, the feature was released with confidence across the board.

Building the skateboard.

This feature was both new and foundational to our platform. Integrating machine learning was something we had not done before. In many ways the feature could be considered a separate product for our company. We knew that this was truly the 'skateboard' we could see becoming the 'bicycle' and then the 'car' in the years ahead. It was a challenge to create new patterns for the experience and anticipate all the ways we would be building off of it in the future.

To meet this challenge, we took a very modular approach to the design and were intentional about how buckets of information were placed. Much of the design is inspired by dashboard concepts that can be rearranged and added to. This initial MVP works with image and text data but we anticipated it working with audio and video in the future. Design decisions were made to accommodate a variety of data types.

All in all, just going into the project understanding that this was an MVP allowed the product and design teams to scope smartly and hold the right kinds of conversations for iteratively improving the feature.

The importance of the onboarding experience.

Because this feature was such a new offering for our users, and one that is naturally complicated, we emphasized onboarding for the experience like never before. A lot of design work was put into onboarding. This helped to polish my skills in designing onboarding experiences.
